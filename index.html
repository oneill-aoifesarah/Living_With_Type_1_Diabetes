About Us

Living with Type 1 diabetes isn't easy, however with the right advice and support, there is no reason why people with diabetes cannot live healthy and fulfilling lives.

Established in 2013, we strive to improve the quality of life for individuals living with Type 1 Diabetes and their families through our network of support services and programmes including:

  • Education Programmes
  • Awareness Campaigns
  • Nutrition and Exercise
  • Support and Guidance

Navigating the volume and complexity of information can often be overwhleming for those newly diagnosed with Type 1 Diabetes. Our website provides clear and accesible information to support individuals living with Type 1 Diabetes in the daily management of their condition.

Our local support groups also provide a space for those living with Type 1 Diabetes to come together and share their experienced in a relaxed and supportive environment.

Advice

Diabetes Management

Type 1 diabetes is a chronic [life-long] autoimmune disease that prevents your pancreas from making insulin.

Daily injections of insulin are needed to keep your blood glucose levels under control.

Checking your blood glucose is quick and easy. This can be done by using a blood glucose monitor

Guidelines suggest that you check your blood glucose regulalry, including the beginning and end of your day. The ideal bloog glucose levels are between 5 to 9 mmol.

Healthy Eating

Knowing how many carbohydrates you're eating and drinking helps you manage your blood glucose levels by matching your fast acting insulin dose to the amount of carbs in your meals and snacks.

Carbohydrates include startchy foods; dairy; sugar; fruits; and honey

Keeping a food diary is useful in learning to track your carbohydrates can be benficial to ensuring you're eating well and avoiding spikes in your blood glucose levels.

Many vegetables, such as carrots, peas, brocoll, and spinach, have a low glycmemic index. This means they have a low carbohydrate count and have minimum impact on blood glucose levels.

Exercise

Regular exercise can help you manage your weight, improve your mood, and contribute to better sleep.

However, strenuous activity can cause your blood sugar to drop and lead to low blood sugar, which is called hypoglycemia.

Intense bouts of exercise can also cause your blood sugar to rise. If it rises above normal levels, its known as hyperglycemia

It's important to check your blood sugar before and after periods of exercise. It's also useful to keep a snack close to hand in the event you experience a drop in your blood sugars.
